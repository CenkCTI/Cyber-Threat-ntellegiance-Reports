# Intelligence Process: Information

## Information in the Intelligence Process

Intelligence Process: Information
Information in the Intelligence Process

Analysts collect lots of data from different sources. More diverse sources can make analysis more accurate but also heavier to handle—sometimes it feels like information overload. Best way: select and classify info. First, understand what information really is.

Path to Strategic Information

Strategic info gives decision-makers general insights, showing cultural, technological, and geographical aspects of conflicts. Two main things matter: how we handle information and how we categorize it.

Approaches to Information

Five ways, but two are not good:

Belief-based: accept without questioning, e.g., superstitions
Authority-based: trust authorities, limited for research

Better approaches:

Rationalist: use logic to infer relations
Observation: careful personal observation
Scientific: systematic collection and analysis, self-correcting

First two should be avoided; others can support scientific approach.

Types of Information

Classify information by type:

Data: raw facts, e.g., number of cars in a parking lot
News: combined data, alone not intelligence
Knowledge: data with meaning, e.g., unemployment up + fewer cars → weak industry
Intelligence: verified and classified info that supports decisions

Also, use both hard data (facts) and soft data (opinions, rumors).

Fundamental Information

Slow-changing info:

Geography, population, social/political structure, education, religious values
These provide a base; dynamic info complements them.
Dynamic Information

Potential powers, political goals, future possibilities. Needs interpretation and imagination.

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)

Sources like newspapers, books, internet, interviews. OSINT is cheap and abundant but can be misleading. Analysts must be critical.

Controlled sources: assigned or questioned
Uncontrolled sources: media, websites, news agencies
