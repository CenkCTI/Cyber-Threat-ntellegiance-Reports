# Comparing, Testing, Evaluating, and Organizing Collected Data, News, and Information

Herbert Spencer said “Science is organized knowledge.” If data is not organized, it is essentially useless for analysis. This stage runs parallel to the collection phase: as intelligence is gathered, it should be compared, tested, and organized to be actionable. Evidence comes from multiple sources, each with strengths, weaknesses, potential biases, and sometimes up-to-date insights. Analysts should control the source, not be dominated by it.

Organizing information goes beyond filing. It involves linking pieces, drawing preliminary conclusions, filling gaps, confirming data, and making sense of the overall picture. This is part of what intelligence literature calls “evaluation.” In the intelligence cycle, evaluation is a critical step. Note that “evaluation” and “valuation” are distinct concepts in intelligence.

A common, though not always efficient, method is to organize information under predefined categories. This approach works well for large and small projects alike. The main advantage is revealing information gaps. Methods include:

- **Verification Method:** Information organized chronologically or by topic, with notes on content.  
- **Card Method:** Notes indicating categories or relationships (e.g., Syria-Army-Tanks). Short and clear.  
- **Maps & Diagrams:** Maps for events’ locations, diagrams for statistics.  
- **Summaries:** Key points on cards, updated periodically for tracking fast developments.  
- **Computer Programs:** Predefined headings to store digital information.

# Types of Data in Intelligence Analysis

Analysts work with data that differs significantly from academic research. Typically, four data types are recognized:

- **Known Facts:** Open-source data, news, and general information.  
- **Secret Info / Puzzle:** Data deliberately protected by states or organizations; puzzle solved by answering specific intelligence questions (e.g., “What is the real range of Iran’s Shahab missiles?”).  
- **Disinformation:** Data from deception operations.  
- **Secrets / Mysterious Data:** Problems that even secret information cannot fully answer. Logic may yield multiple equally likely solutions. Often pertains to political or operational questions (e.g., “Where/when will a terrorist group attack in Turkey?”).

---

# Solving Secrets / Full-Spectrum Analysis

Adrian Wolberg advocates full-spectrum analysis to address such mysteries. Traditional intelligence often treated the world as a static puzzle of secrets, which is insufficient. Human behavior is dynamic. Wolberg’s approach: expand the analytical horizon, focus on individual secrets, iterate hypotheses, broaden possibilities, and progressively narrow conclusions.

# Source Evaluation

General Gehlen stated: “One report is not a report until verified by another independent source.” Source evaluation is essential:

- **A:** Extremely reliable; directly observed.  
- **B:** Trusted source with a known record.  
- **C:** Requires repeated verification before considered reliable.

Even highly reliable sources can err. Historical cases demonstrate that information can degrade or become distorted during transmission.

---

# Evaluating News

When evaluating news, analysts should consider the source, prior performance, and potential biases. Even reputable agencies (Reuters, IMF, BBC, etc.) require verification. Government statistics or official reports may be misleading and should be cross-checked.

Key considerations:

- Information can degrade from the original source to the final recipient.  
- Form (delivery) is not equal to content; a correctly delivered report may still contain inaccurate details.  
- First-hand, live intelligence often holds more value than secondary or statistical reports.

---

# Comparative Organization of Data

Organizing and comparing data is straightforward but time-intensive. Early effort pays off in faster, more accurate analysis later. Analysts should identify missing elements, formulate alternative hypotheses, and assess whether gaps are due to normal limitations or collection failures.

---

# CTI Note-Style Summary

- Organize, test, and cross-check sources.  
- Recognize data types: known, secret/puzzle, disinformation, mysterious.  
- Use full-spectrum analysis: expand horizon, focus, iterate.  
- Evaluate sources using reliability grades (A, B, C).  
- Critically evaluate news: check origin, transmission, and biases.  
- Comparative organization highlights gaps and accelerates analysis.  
- Maintain skepticism; never rely on a single source.
